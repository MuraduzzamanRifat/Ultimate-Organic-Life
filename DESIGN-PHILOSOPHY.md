# Field Study

**A design philosophy for the storefront as botanical specimen archive.**

---

## Manifesto

Every organic good is an artifact. Before it was a listing, it was a living thing — pulled from soil, touched by weather, carried by hands that knew its names. A storefront that reduces such a thing to a grid tile and a price commits a quiet violence. *Field Study* refuses that compression. It treats the commercial surface as a field journal: each product a pressed specimen, each category a chapter in an ongoing catalogue, each transaction a small act of custodianship. The screen becomes paper; the scroll becomes a turning of pages. What is sold is never *only* sold. It is also noticed, labeled, admired.

The visual language borrows from the sober elegance of 19th-century natural-history volumes, the taxonomic precision of Linnaean plates, the unhurried confidence of the Japanese photobook, and the structural restraint of Swiss grid systems. Nothing is decorative. Every mark, every rule, every margin exists because a practiced eye placed it there. This is meant to feel like the work of someone who has studied the craft for decades — the product of countless passes, each refinement smaller than the last, each revision a closer approximation of an invisible ideal. The result should read as hand-set, not generated; considered, not assembled.

## Space and Form

The page breathes. Generous asymmetric margins on the outer edges frame the composition the way a matting frames a print — never flush, never cramped. The underlying grid is twelve columns but it is violated often and on purpose: a single image may span seven columns while text sits narrow in three, a specimen may drift into the gutter to break the rhythm. Empty space is not waste; it is the air around the subject. White (or its warm cream variant) carries as much weight as any image, and the silence between elements does the work that decoration would do in a lesser design. Forms are clean-edged, their geometry simple: circles that frame, rules that separate, rectangles that catalogue. No gradients, no soft shadows, no glass; the medium is paper and ink.

## Color and Material

The palette is drawn from herbarium pressings: a living leaf-green for the active voice, a clear field-sky blue for clarity and counterpoint, a deep forest-ink for the text and typographic weight, set against a warm paper cream that reads like archival stock. Accent chromatics are used sparingly — a single desaturated pop per section, never a chord of competing hues. The material sensibility is flat, matte, dry. No digital sheen. Textures are restrained: a faint paper grain beneath hero images, a single hairline rule under a heading, a letterpress-style emboss only where an authentic printed piece would justify it. The craft is in the calibration, not the effect.

## Scale, Rhythm, and Hierarchy

Typographic scale is dramatic and disciplined. Display serifs swell to editorial size — used once per spread, never twice. They are paired with a small, geometric sans for meta information and a tight monospace for specimen codes, catalogue numbers, and Latin names (used with a quiet wink, not reverent pedantry). Body text holds a calm mid-size at a generous measure, set in a humanist serif that welcomes sustained reading. Rhythm is established by alternating dense content blocks with near-empty passages — a loud page is followed by a quiet one, as in a well-edited photobook. Scale carries the hierarchy; color and weight do the minor tuning. Information is layered: a large image, a small specimen label, a tiny price, each earning attention at its own distance.

## Composition and Voice

Layouts are asymmetric and editorial. Product cards are not identical tiles but specimen entries of varying size and crop — a pair of apples may be presented full-bleed opposite a modest paragraph of provenance; a jar of honey may sit alone at center, haloed by margin. Navigation behaves like a running head: always present, never shouting. Text is used as a visual instrument: a pull quote in display size, a caption in mono, a set of annotations handwritten into the margin. Copy is sparse, deliberate, and occasionally dry — the voice of a patient curator, not a marketer. Numbers — volume, issue, year, specimen count — become part of the composition, the way page numbers belong to a book.

## Craftsmanship, Always

This is not a theme. It is not a template adapted. It is meant to feel like the quiet confidence of a practice that has been refined over years, where every apparent simplicity hides a hundred rejected alternatives. The final impression should be of a thing made slowly and without shortcut — the product of deep expertise, painstaking attention, and master-level execution. A visitor should sense, even without naming it, that someone cared enough to line the rules up to the pixel, to tune the letter-spacing, to weigh the margins against the image. The goal is that the storefront feels less like a webpage and more like a volume pulled from a library shelf — something worth keeping, something that could only have been made by someone at the top of their field.
