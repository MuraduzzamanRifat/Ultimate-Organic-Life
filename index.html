Field Note nº 014

Filed from Ortega Farm, Sonoma Co.
By P. Mensah

“We harvest on the morning the sky tells us to. The calendar is a suggestion; the weather is the boss.”

— Ana Ortega, third-generation grower, over coffee at 6:14 a.m.

Four generations, one valley, and an argument about soil.

The Ortega farm sits in a crease between two hills that the Mission Blue butterfly also happens to favour. Ana’s grandfather first planted there in 1953, on land that had been considered too rocky to be useful. The family took their first certified-organic crop to market in 1984, at a time when the word still confused buyers, and have not looked back since.

What makes their produce peculiar — a word Ana uses with obvious affection — is a soil amendment programme that has been refined over seventy years of trial, argument, and the occasional shouting match at the dinner table. The result is a tomato that tastes of sunshine and a little of iron, a courgette with a hollow resonance when tapped, and a squash blossom so fragile it must be packed in single layers and driven directly.
